I want to share the story of my educational journey—how I've spent so many years since high school graduation and am still in school. Despite the twists and turns, I have no regrets.

## Early Education in Malaysia

My story begins with my high school studies at an international school in Malaysia. I attended from the second year all the way through 10th grade. However, things became complicated around 10th grade due to my family's financial troubles. We faced tough decisions about whether I could continue at the international school, which had high tuition fees, or return to Korea to attend public school, which would cost far less or even be free.

## Transition to the Korean Education System

In 10th grade, I finally decided to go back to Korea. Unlike many countries where the school year starts in September, schools in Korea (and Japan) start in March. My international school in Malaysia began in September and ended around July. When I finished 10th grade and returned to Korea in September, Korean schools were starting their second semester of 10th grade instead of the first.

Adapting to the Korean education system was sudden and challenging. I had been studying for IGCSEs and A-levels under the British education system, and now I had to adjust to the Korean system. Additionally, I hadn't read or studied in Korean for a very long time, which added to the difficulty.

## Academic Challenges

Starting in the second semester meant I missed the entire first semester of classes. As a result, my GPA wasn't great. The Korean GPA system is relative, with grades ranging from 1 to 9. Achieving a grade of 1 means you're in the top 4% of your class, while a grade of 3 places you in the top 33%. Because of my late start and language challenges, I initially ranked around the top 33%, which isn't sufficient to gain admission to prestigious universities in Korea.

In subsequent semesters, I worked hard and eventually became one of the top four students in my school. This improved my average GPA from 3.5 to around 1.8 (with 1 being the best). However, this was still not good enough for many top universities.

## University Admissions and Setbacks

I applied to several prestigious universities but was rejected by all of them. Only one university showed interest but ultimately rejected me after the interview. In Korea, we can apply to a maximum of six universities, and there's only one application window per year. Unlike in the U.S., where students can start in different semesters, in Korea, you can only begin in the spring semester.

Having been rejected by all the schools, I had to wait another year to apply again—another year added to my educational journey.

## Acceptance and Pursuing Biology

Interestingly, I reapplied to the same university that had previously rejected me after the interview. This time, I chose the option to not take the interview, and they accepted me. I enrolled in biotech engineering, specifically stem cell and regenerative biotechnology. Biology was my passion. I wanted to become a professor or researcher to solve problems in agriculture and the food industry—areas I cared deeply about.

## Losing Interest in Biology

However, during the year I spent preparing again for university entrance exams, I lost much of my interest and motivation in biology. My passion for biology had stemmed from constantly learning new things, and the prolonged period of studying for exams without new material took its toll. Although I tried to learn independently, there were limits without access to university-level education.

## Discovering a Passion for Programming

When I entered the university, they mandated that science majors study Python programming. I was supposed to take a course, but due to limited slots—only enough for about 10% of new students—I couldn't enroll. So, I studied Python on my own, and it was the most fun I'd ever had studying.

I've always been interested in programming and engineering. During high school at the international school, I planned to pursue engineering. I thought virtual reality was cool, mechatronics fascinated me, and I wanted to create Minecraft mods. I loved making new things.

I shifted to biology because I recognized serious problems in the world and wanted to help solve them. Going through a phase of depression, I sought purpose and found it in what biology could do to help the world. As I overcame depression, I realized there's more to life, and even with my noble reasons for choosing biology, it didn't matter if programming was more enjoyable to study.

## An Internal Conflict

This realization led to a conflict. I felt obligated to continue with biology—I had spent four years trying to get into a prestigious university for it—but my interest had shifted to programming. I worried that this might be a fleeting passion that would fade like my interest in biology. So, I decided to stay in biology for a year to see how it went. I took electives related to programming and continued to study on my own.

## Military Service and Making a Decision

The following year, on February 11th, I enlisted in the army. In Korea, men are required to serve approximately one and a half years of mandatory military service. This period was another opportunity to contemplate whether to continue studying computer science or return to biology. However, it also meant I wasn't continuing my formal education during that time.

There was no choice regarding military service unless I left the country, which wasn't a viable option and would have legal consequences. So, on top of everything, my education would be delayed further. If I pursued computer science, I would lose another year due to the need to transfer majors and catch up on prerequisites.

Despite these challenges, I decided during my army service to transfer my major to computer science. I applied for the transfer and waited.

## Conclusion

Although my educational path has been long and winding, with delays and changes in direction, I have no regrets. Each experience has taught me valuable lessons and led me to where I am today.
